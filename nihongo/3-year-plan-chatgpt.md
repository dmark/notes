# A 3-year Plan

Multi-year plans are kinda bogus. Everything changes. Years 3 - 5 of a 5-year plan are completely useless. That said, here's what ChatGPT suggested a 5-year plan to achieve N3 or even N2.

I am applying to, and hope to do a Master's program in cybersecurity, focussing on digital trust and identity. Part-time, that program will likely take me about 3 years (10 academic terms), assuming I can do one course, on credit, per academic term, including the Summer term.

---

**Short answer:** Yes—**N3 is very achievable**, and **N2 is plausible** within three years **even while doing a part-time Master’s**, *if* you keep Japanese on a low-friction daily cadence.

Below is a sober, experience-based take.

## The target, translated into reality

By **Summer 2029** (≈36 months), aiming for:

* **JLPT N3**: *very likely*
* **JLPT N2**: *realistic but conditional*

The deciding factor is **consistency**, not intelligence, age, or “language talent”.

## What N3 and N2 actually mean for you

### N3 (baseline professional viability)

At N3 you can:

* follow meetings with context + support
* read internal emails and tickets (dictionary-assisted)
* communicate clearly but simply
* reassure Japan-based hiring managers

For foreign Tier-1 tech firms in Japan, **N3 + senior IAM expertise = hireable**.

### N2 (comfort + credibility)

At N2 you can:

* handle most meetings without translation
* read specs, proposals, and documentation
* operate independently in Japanese business settings

For AWS / Microsoft / Okta Japan, **N2 removes language as a risk factor**.

## Time math (this matters)

Three years = ~1,000 days.

You do **not** need heroic study.
You need **~45 minutes/day average**.

That yields:

* ~750–800 focused study hours
* which historically maps to:

  * strong N3
  * weak-to-solid N2 for disciplined adult learners

Your prior exposure and professional motivation reduce friction further.

## How the Master’s degree interacts (not conflicts)

Your plan of **one course per term** is actually ideal.

### During academic terms

* Maintain a **maintenance rhythm**:

  * Anki (10–15 min)
  * light reading/listening (20–30 min)

### Between terms / lighter periods

* Push grammar
* Increase speaking
* Do mock JLPT prep

Japanese becomes **a long-running background process**, not a competing project.

## A realistic 3-year Japanese trajectory

### Year 1 (now → Summer 2027)

**Goal:** Solid N4, early N3

* Finish **Genki I** and **Genki II**
* Begin **Tobira**
* 600–800 vocab, ~300–400 kanji
* Start speaking regularly

👉 You are no longer “learning basics”; you are *operating*.

### Year 2 (Summer 2027 → Summer 2028)

**Goal:** Pass N3

* Finish Tobira
* Daily reading (graded → semi-native)
* Weekly speaking focused on:

  * explaining systems
  * customer-style conversations
* Sit N3 once (or twice, if needed)

👉 Recruiters now take you seriously.

### Year 3 (Summer 2028 → Summer 2029)

**Goal:** Push toward N2

* N2 grammar consolidation
* Heavy listening (meetings, news, tech talks)
* Reading professional material
* Optional N2 attempt

👉 Even if you fall short of N2, your **functional Japanese will exceed “N3 on paper.”**

That’s what matters.

## Age realism (important, and honest)

At 54:

* you may memorize vocab slightly slower
* but you:

  * self-regulate better
  * quit less often
  * apply language immediately to real goals

Adult professionals routinely outperform younger learners **past N3** because they stop “studying Japanese” and start **using it**.

## My professional judgment

Given:

* your discipline
* your IAM seniority
* your realistic expectations
* your 3-year horizon

**N3 by 2029 is almost a foregone conclusion.**
**N2 is achievable if you maintain momentum after N3 instead of coasting.**

## One strategic suggestion (optional but powerful)

Plan to **sit N3 earlier than needed** (e.g., Year 2), not “just in time.”

That frees Year 3 to:

* improve spoken fluency
* read more
* align Japanese with job interviews

Certificates open doors; **fluency closes deals**.
